It’s Not Too Late to Start

October 16, 2025

I grew up being good at math and science. The kind of good that makes teachers say things like, “You’ve got real talent.”

But here’s the thing about talent — it just means you learn faster. It doesn’t mean you love it more. It doesn’t mean it fills you up.

Ever since I can remember, I’ve had this itch to create — to draw, play the guitar, build model kits, make jewelry, film goofy videos, write stories, DM campaigns… anything that let me make something from nothing. But I let that itch fester in the background while I focused on what I thought were the “right” things — the things that were supposed to make me successful.

Back in my final year at Estero High School (oh-eight, ain’t it great?), I literally set the curve in Mr. Mitchell’s physics class — somewhere around 125%. (Sorry, everyone — I love you!) I aced every question, even the extra credit. Talent. Sure. But my dream wasn’t to be a scientist. I wanted to be a 3D animator — maybe even work on a new Star Wars movie someday.

But when I got into the University of Florida, I learned they didn’t have an animation program yet. Strike one.
Then I told myself, “Well, they’re probably done making Star Wars movies anyway, now that Episode III is out.” Oh, sweet summer child. Strike two.
And then I gave myself strike three — for no real reason at all — and benched my dream.

So I did what seemed logical. I sat down with my dad, made a spreadsheet of every major at UF, and sorted by three columns: average starting salary, difficulty, and credit hours. Chemical Engineering came out on top for every single metric. It checked every rational box.
Prerequisites: be good at chemistry and even better at math. Check and check. Let’s do it.

I didn’t even know what an engineer did for work, but it seemed like a challenge — and I can never resist a challenge. (I honestly didn’t figure out what a day in the life of an engineer looked like until my summer internship in 2012.)

But I stuck with Chemical Engineering. Even graduated with honors. And to be fair, it’s brought me plenty of fulfillment over the years — just not the kind that makes your soul come alive.

Then something shifted on my 33rd birthday.

It hit me that it’s not too late.
Not too late to start.
Not too late to follow that tug on your soul.
Not too late to tell the stories you’ve kept bottled up because “life got in the way.”

So here I am — an engineer turned aspiring author — writing my first novel.

And maybe this post is just my way of saying: if there’s something calling you, something you’ve convinced yourself it’s too late for… it’s not. You’re not.

The only real deadline is the one where you stop believing you can begin.
